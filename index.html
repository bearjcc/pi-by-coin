Estimating π with a Coin

Flip until heads lead, record the fraction. The average tends to π/4.

Did you know that you can calculate the value of pi by flipping coins? We flip until heads first lead, record the fraction of heads at that moment; the average of that fraction is π/4, so 4×(fraction) estimates π. Try it with the simulator below; learn more about it here.

Why does this work?

Probability and flipping coins

We assume a fair coin: each flip is heads with probability 1/2 and tails with probability 1/2, and flips are independent. So the sequence you see is random in the sense that no flip influences another. We stop the first time the total number of heads is greater than the total number of tails, and we record the fraction of flips that were heads at that moment (e.g. 3 heads out of 5 flips gives 3/5). That fraction is always greater than 1/2 by construction.

Catalan numbers

The Catalan number counts, among other things, the number of paths that stay at or below zero for the first steps and end at zero (like a ballot where tails never lead).
